Statlet: Exploring Probabilities—Build a Slot Machine

Note:

The goal: develop intuitions about probabilities, their distributions, and how probabilities combine into joint probabilities. This statlet's underlying dataset is an excerpt from the 2013 causes-of-death counts across all US States (CDC: Center for Disease Control).

You will build a slot machine that allows you to bet on the cause of one or more upcoming US deaths. Each slot module of your machine comprises four components:

  • A white spin-outcome window.
  • A set of buttons that spin the module once, 10, or 100 times in a row. Each spin is one draw from the cause-of-death probabilities.
  • A histogram of spin outcomes, and
  • A pulldown menu to place a bet on the next outcome.
The green number counts your number of wins.

The chart on the far right shows the probability of a heart attack compared to a stroke. This initial value of 0.5 for both does not reflect the true data. We'll get to the truth in future steps. This part is just to get you started. The chart shows a probability distribution: the bars show how probabilities are distributed across the causes of death. For now, let's assume there are only two ways to die.

Things to do:

  • Hit the Go buttons.
  • Place a bet; then spin again.
  • Be the casino owner (or God): lower/raise the probability bars. then spin again a bunch of times.
Things to wonder about:
  • Add the probabilities in the distribution. What's the sum? What is the range of values this sum can take?
  • Observing how the histogram changes as you spin, compare its shape with the shape of the cause-of-death distribution.
  • Spin a few times, observing the histogram. Then change one of the two probability bars and spin a few more times. How are the changes in the histogram different before and after that manipulation of the probability?
  • When you change one of the probabilities in the distribution you'll notice the other bar going the opposite direction. Why is that?
By the way: you can drag the slot module to a convenient location. You'll find that you want to in a bit.
Let's make the machine more interesting. You now see a new button: Add Slot Module. Click it. You want to, don't you? The result will be a second slot module. If you didn't move the one you already have, the new and old modules will be on top of each other. No worries. Drag the new module to the right of the original module until the new module turns green. Let go of the mouse, and the two modules dock. You now have a two-module slot machine. You can drag it around. Note that you can place different bets in each module. Better than Vegas!

Notice the dock connector labeled and. It tells you that to win, both of your bets must be winners. Try it: make a bet on both modules. Spin a bunch. You will see the score board change only when both slots win.

You've doubtlessly noticed that a formula now appears when you slide the mouse over one of the slots. You recognize that formula: it computes the joint probability across the slot modules.

To wonder about (and to satisfy your boundless curiosity about statistics):

  • What would happen if the and in the dock connector were an or? Specifically: How would the rate of winning change?
  • Wonder enough about it? OK, select the dock connector, choose or, and try it out.
  • After spinning a slot a lot, driving up the bars of the module's histogram, you sometimes see the Y-axis change. Why is it doing that?
Let's play with a fuller death deck. These 21 selected causes of death are a sample of the 5904 that the CDC lists as options for us to go from. One that I left out: Cardiovascular devices associated with adverse incidents, prosthetic and other implants, materials and accessory devices (two 2013 cases in the US).

The probability distribution is more complex, but nothing else changes. Same principles. Adding the probabilities would sum to...what? Adjusting a probability upwards would cause the other bars to do...what? Yep, same thing as in Step 1. Build as complex a slot machine as you wish, place your bets, and watch the win rate.

Now, casino patrons don't get to modify whether slots in one-armed bandits are coupled with ands versus ors. But casino owners can do anything they like, subject to gambling regulations.

To wonder about:

  • Say you are a casino owner. Your goal is to make money (i.e. to have patrons lose a lot). But you also want to ensure that patrons are not discouraged. They should win some of the time. Just qualitatively, how would you combine manipulating death cause probabilities with and/or dock connector settings to achieve one or the other of these goals?
  • In a 2015 article, Healthline.com reported:
    New research finds that chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) increases the risk for sudden cardiac death, even among patients without major heart problems.
    That finding is bad news for this statlet. How does this finding invalidate the application's probability calculations?
